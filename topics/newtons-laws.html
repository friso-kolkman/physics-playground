The Physics

Newton's first law says objects are lazy: a thing at rest stays at rest, and a thing in motion keeps moving at the same speed in the same direction, unless a force comes along and changes that. This is inertia, and mass is the measure of how much an object resists changes to its motion.

The second law puts a number on it. Force equals mass times acceleration (F = ma). Push a shopping cart and it flies forward. Push a truck with the same effort and it barely moves. Same force, different mass, different acceleration. It is the most useful equation in all of mechanics.

The third law says forces always come in pairs. When you push the wall, the wall pushes you back with exactly the same force. When a rocket blasts exhaust downward, the exhaust pushes the rocket upward. No force exists in isolation.

Math Details

Newton's second law in its simplest form:

F = ma   →   a = F / m

With friction opposing the motion (coefficient μ):

Fnet = Fapplied - μmg

Kinematic equations for constant acceleration:

v = v0 + at x = x0 + v0t + ½at²

Beyond Physics

Inertia in organizations. Large companies are high-mass objects. They have thousands of employees, entrenched processes, and legacy systems. Changing direction requires enormous, sustained force. This is not a bug; it is physics. Small nudges produce negligible acceleration.

F = ma for startups. A five-person team is a low-mass system. Apply the same force (effort, capital, focus) and you get dramatically more acceleration. This is the structural advantage of being small, not just culture, but physics.

Friction as bureaucracy. Every approval layer, every sign-off chain, every compliance check adds friction to the system. The net force propelling work forward shrinks. Sometimes friction is necessary (you do want brakes on a car), but unexamined friction quietly kills momentum.
