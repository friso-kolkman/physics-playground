The Physics

The ideal gas law, PV = nRT, links pressure, volume, temperature, and particle count into a single elegant equation. It emerges from kinetic theory: gas pressure is simply the aggregate of countless molecular collisions against the container walls. Every particle bouncing off a surface delivers a tiny impulse; sum them all up and you get a measurable, predictable force.

Boyle's Law says that at constant temperature, pressure and volume are inversely proportional — compress a gas and the particles hit the walls more often. Charles's Law says that at constant pressure, volume grows with temperature — hotter particles move faster and push the walls outward.

The path matters. In an isothermal process (constant temperature), the gas exchanges heat with its surroundings to maintain equilibrium — compression is gradual and reversible. In an adiabatic process (no heat exchange), compression heats the gas and expansion cools it. The same start and end states can be reached by different paths, but the work done depends on which path you take.

Math Details

The ideal gas law:

PV = nRT

Kinetic pressure from molecular motion (average over many particles):

P = ⅓ ρ v²

Boyle's Law — constant temperature:

P&sub1;V&sub1; = P&sub2;V&sub2;

Adiabatic process (no heat exchange):

PVγ = const

Where γ is the heat capacity ratio (5/3 for monatomic ideal gas).

Beyond Physics

PV=nRT for Organizations. Pressure (urgency), volume (scope), temperature (energy/resources), and particle count (team size) are linked. You can't increase scope without either adding people, raising urgency, or pouring in more energy. Reduce scope and you relieve pressure. The equation always balances — pretending otherwise is how teams burn out.

The Path Matters. Isothermal change is gradual — the system stays in equilibrium at every step. Adiabatic change is sudden — no time for heat exchange, and internal temperature spikes. Reorganizations, pivots, layoffs: the same destination reached slowly (with communication) vs. suddenly (without) produces very different outcomes. The journey shapes the destination.

Pressure Comes from Collisions. Gas pressure isn't one big force — it's the aggregate of millions of tiny molecular impacts. Organizational pressure works the same way. It's not one massive crisis; it's the accumulation of small interactions: Slack pings, context switches, unclear ownership, meetings about meetings. Fix the micro-collisions and the macro-pressure drops.
