The Physics

Momentum is mass times velocity. It is always conserved in collisions, meaning the total momentum of the system before a collision equals the total momentum after. This comes directly from Newton's third law: when two objects collide, they exert equal and opposite forces on each other for the same duration, producing equal and opposite impulses.

Kinetic energy, however, is only conserved in elastic collisions. In inelastic collisions, some kinetic energy converts to heat, sound, and deformation. In a perfectly inelastic collision, the objects stick together and move as one. The kinetic energy lost is maximized in this case, but momentum is still perfectly conserved.

Math Details

Momentum of a single object:

p = mv

Total momentum is conserved in all collisions:

m1v1 + m2v2 = m1v1' + m2v2' = constant

For elastic collisions, kinetic energy is also conserved:

½m1v1² + ½m2v2² = ½m1v1'² + ½m2v2'²

Solving both equations simultaneously gives the elastic collision result:

v1' = ((m1 - m2)v1 + 2m2v2) / (m1 + m2)

For a perfectly inelastic collision, the objects stick together:

v' = (m1v1 + m2v2) / (m1 + m2)

Beyond Physics

The Momentum Equation. Momentum = mass x velocity. A large slow company has the same momentum as a small fast one. When planning competitive strategy, don't just look at speed (velocity) â€” multiply by mass (resources, market share, brand). The slow incumbent may carry more momentum than you think.

Elastic vs Inelastic Outcomes. In elastic collisions, both parties survive but change direction. In inelastic collisions, they merge and energy is lost to heat. M&A is inelastic: two companies merge, lose energy to integration costs, but total momentum is conserved. Partnerships are elastic: both parties bounce off and keep moving, changed but intact.

Impulse: Force x Time. The same momentum change can come from a huge force briefly (a crisis pivot) or a small force over a long time (compound habits). Both change your trajectory equally. Dramatic pivots get attention, but consistent small pushes are mathematically equivalent and far less risky.
