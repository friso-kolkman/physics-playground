The Physics

Momentum is mass times velocity. It is always conserved in collisions, meaning the total momentum of the system before a collision equals the total momentum after. This comes directly from Newton's third law: when two objects collide, they exert equal and opposite forces on each other for the same duration, producing equal and opposite impulses.

Kinetic energy, however, is only conserved in elastic collisions. In inelastic collisions, some kinetic energy converts to heat, sound, and deformation. In a perfectly inelastic collision, the objects stick together and move as one. The kinetic energy lost is maximized in this case, but momentum is still perfectly conserved.

Math Details

Momentum of a single object:

p = mv

Total momentum is conserved in all collisions:

m1v1 + m2v2 = m1v1' + m2v2' = constant

For elastic collisions, kinetic energy is also conserved:

½m1v1² + ½m2v2² = ½m1v1'² + ½m2v2'²

Solving both equations simultaneously gives the elastic collision result:

v1' = ((m1 - m2)v1 + 2m2v2) / (m1 + m2)

For a perfectly inelastic collision, the objects stick together:

v' = (m1v1 + m2v2) / (m1 + m2)

Beyond Physics

Momentum in negotiations. A heavy stakeholder (high mass) moving slowly still carries enormous momentum. Do not underestimate slow-moving incumbents. Their inertia means they are hard to stop once they start moving in a direction.

Elastic vs inelastic in competition. Some market collisions are elastic: both players bounce off and keep going, maybe in different directions. Others are inelastic (acquisitions): they merge and move as one. The acquirer absorbs the target's momentum. Energy is always lost in the merge, but the combined entity carries the full momentum forward.

Conservation law for teams. Total team momentum equals the sum of individual efforts times their velocity. One person stopping does not just remove their contribution; it changes the system's total momentum. And in an inelastic team collision (a conflict that sticks), energy gets lost to friction.
