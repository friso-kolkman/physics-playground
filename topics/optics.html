  • Switch between convex lens and concave mirror. Same convergence pattern, completely different mechanism.

The Physics

Light bends when it passes through a lens (refraction) and bounces when it hits a mirror (reflection). The thin lens equation relates the object distance, image distance, and focal length into one elegant rule. A convex lens converges parallel light to a focal point. A concave lens diverges it, making rays appear to originate from a virtual focal point behind the lens.

Real images form where rays actually converge — you can project them onto a screen. Virtual images form where rays only appear to diverge from — you see them by looking through the optic, but no light actually reaches that point. Three principal rays are enough to locate any image: one parallel to the axis, one through the center, and one through (or toward) the focal point.

Mirrors follow the same mathematics but reflect instead of refract. A concave mirror acts like a convex lens (converging), and a convex mirror acts like a concave lens (diverging). The sign conventions flip for mirrors because the image forms on the same side as the object.

Math Details

The thin lens (and mirror) equation:

1/f = 1/do + 1/di

Magnification — the ratio of image height to object height:

M = −di / do

Sign conventions (lenses): f > 0 for convex, f < 0 for concave. di > 0 means real image (opposite side), di < 0 means virtual image (same side).

Sign conventions (mirrors): f > 0 for concave, f < 0 for convex. di > 0 means real image (same side as object), di < 0 means virtual image (behind mirror).

|M| > 1 means magnified, |M| < 1 means diminished. M < 0 means inverted.

Beyond Physics

The Lens Effect. A convex lens takes scattered parallel light and focuses it into a single burning point. Great leaders do the same — they take scattered effort, competing priorities, and diffuse energy, and focus them into a single point of impact. The tighter the focus, the more intense the result. Defocused effort feels busy but burns nothing.

Focal Length Sets Your Range. A short focal length gives you a close, magnified view — the specialist. A long focal length gives you a wide, smaller view — the generalist. Neither is better; they solve different problems. The mistake is using a telephoto lens when you need a microscope, or vice versa. Know your focal length.

Real vs Virtual Images. Real images can be projected onto a screen — they exist where light actually converges. Virtual images look real but no light actually reaches that point. Revenue, shipped products, signed contracts — those are real images. Vanity metrics, pitch decks, and projections — those are virtual images. Know which one you are measuring.
